The Look That Lasts

Film photography remains a quiet act of faith. You compose. You expose. And then you wait. The magic, to me, is that film doesn’t just capture the look of a thing. It captures the light that one physically touched it. That light, refracted off someone’s cheekbone, caught in the curve of a smile, pouring across the spine of a denim jacket, is imprinted chemically, not digitally. It's real. It's reactive. It's honest. Unlike digital, where you can shoot a hundred frames to chase perfection, film reminds you that one is enough. If it’s the right one. You learn to be intentional. You become attuned to time, texture, silence. You notice how someone shifts when they think you’re not looking. You fall in love with what’s fleeting.

What I love about working with people, especially in portraiture, is that film encourages presence. There’s no screen for constant checking, no instant feedback loop. It’s just me, the subject, and the moment unfolding between us. That space becomes sacred. A kind of shared trust builds when you both understand this might be it. This is the shot. And even when the photo comes back imperfect, soft focus, light leak, motion blur, there’s a kind of grace in it. You remember how it felt. You don’t fix it. You accept it. Sometimes, those are the images that stay with me the longest. Film isn’t about control. It’s about connection. It reminds us that beauty lives in the unrepeatable, that the most lasting looks are often the ones we don’t plan for. So I keep shooting. I keep waiting. And I keep learning what the light is trying to tell me.

There’s also something beautifully democratic about film. The same 36 frames you load into your camera are the same ones someone else is using halfway across the world. There’s no algorithm favoring one image over another, no filter pack to save a flat shot. Just light, chemistry, and choice. It levels the playing field, asking only that you pay attention. In a world obsessed with speed and polish, film slows you down—not out of nostalgia, but out of necessity. You become more present, more aware of the moment you’re living inside. It’s in that slowness that intimacy grows. With your subject, yes, but also with yourself. Film invites you to get comfortable with not knowing. You won’t see how it turned out right away. You won’t get to tweak and reshoot and curate instantly. You have to trust your instincts. And over time, those instincts sharpen. You begin to rely on your eye, your gut, the feeling in your chest when you press the shutter. That kind of trust is rare—and in a way, it’s the real gift film gives you.

In this way, film becomes not just a medium, but a method for being present. It teaches you to live inside the moment instead of trying to capture it perfectly. When I shoot portraits, I’m not chasing perfection. I’m watching for something quieter: the moment someone lets go of how they want to be seen, and simply is. There’s a kind of alchemy that happens in that in-between space—after the posed smile fades but before the guard comes back up. A flicker of realness, unedited, unrepeatable. That’s the shot I want. Not because it’s polished, but because it’s true. And with film, that truth has weight. It lives in the grain, in the slight softness, in the imperfections that digital would try to smooth over. It’s emotional residue. A fingerprint of how it felt to be there. That’s the look that lasts.

And maybe that’s why film still feels necessary to me, even now, when there are faster, cleaner, more convenient ways to make an image. Because we don’t always need cleaner. We need truer. We need reminders that beauty isn’t always in what’s flawless—it’s in what’s felt. Film slows us down, not out of nostalgia, but out of reverence. It demands that we be human. That we trust the process, and each other. It encourages patience, imperfection, vulnerability. And in return, it gives us something rare: a record not just of what was in front of the camera, but of everything that surrounded it. The breath held before the click. The laughter just after. The quiet awe of knowing that, for a single fraction of a second, everything aligned. And then it was gone. Except now, somehow, it isn’t.
